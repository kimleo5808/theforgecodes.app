## How Narutodle Works

[Narutodle](https://narutodle.app/) gives you a blank grid with **nine attribute columns** and asks you to identify the day's hidden character. The columns track:

1. Gender
2. Clan
3. Village
4. Ninja rank
5. Jutsu types
6. Nature releases
7. Kekkei genkai
8. Special attributes
9. Debut arc

After each guess, every cell is color-coded: **green** = exact match, **yellow** = partial overlap, **red** = no match. For the debut arc, an arrow tells you whether the answer appears earlier or later. The same character is featured worldwide each day and resets at UTC midnight, so everyone is solving the same puzzle.

The character pool is **210 Shippūden-era characters** (no Boruto), and there's full alias support — typing "Kakashi-sensei" works fine.

## The Core Principle: Maximize Coverage

The single most important idea in Narutodle is **information per guess**. A good guess isn't the character you think is the answer — it's the character that *splits the roster* most evenly no matter what the answer is.

That means your opening guesses should touch as many distinct attribute values as possible. A character with a common village, a unique clan, multiple jutsu types, and a mid-table debut arc gives you feedback across the whole grid at once. A super-obscure character with a rare everything tells you almost nothing when it comes back all red.

## Best Opening Guesses

You want openers that are **high-coverage** — touching four or more columns distinctively. Strong opening characteristics:

- **A mainstream village** (Konoha) so a green/red on village immediately halves the pool
- **Multiple nature releases**, which is one of the most discriminating columns
- **A well-known clan** rather than "no clan," since clan is a fast eliminator
- **A mid-range debut arc**, so the arrow points you in a useful direction either way

A common, well-documented main-cast ninja is almost always a better opener than a fan-favorite side character. Save the deep cuts for when you've already narrowed the field.

## Reading the Grid Like a Pro

Here's the read order that wastes the fewest guesses:

1. **Start with the binary/low-cardinality columns** — gender, village, rank. A green here removes the largest chunks of the roster instantly.
2. **Use the debut-arc arrow aggressively.** It's a directional clue, so two guesses can bracket the answer's era and eliminate everyone outside it.
3. **Treat yellow as a strong lead, not a near-miss.** A yellow on jutsu types or nature releases means the answer *shares* one value — chase characters that overlap on exactly that attribute.
4. **Cross-reference, don't tunnel.** Don't fixate on matching one green column; pick your next guess so it tests two or three *unknown* columns at once.

## Silhouette Mode Tips

Narutodle also has a **Silhouette** daily mode: a darkened character portrait that brightens with each of your ten wrong guesses. Strategy here is different — it's pattern recognition, not deduction.

- **Look at the outline first**, before color reveals. Hair shape and silhouette are the biggest tells in Naruto's roster.
- **Don't burn guesses early** just to brighten the image; the edges alone often identify spiky vs. long vs. styled hair.
- **Scars, masks, and headgear** (Kakashi's mask, Gaara's gourd, Madara's hair) are giveaways once the first brightening stage hits.

## Build the Habit Without Losing Your Streak

Streaks are the addictive part of any daily game, and the easiest way to keep one alive is to attach it to a habit you already have. A clean five-minute daily loop:

1. **Check your codes first.** Open the latest The Forge codes, redeem anything active, note the timestamp.
2. **Solve Narutodle Classic.** Lead with a high-coverage opener, read the low-cardinality columns first.
3. **Do the Silhouette round** if you want a second puzzle.
4. **Share your emoji grid** with friends and turn it into a daily race.

Because [Narutodle](https://narutodle.app/) saves your streak locally with no login, the only thing standing between you and a long streak is remembering to play — which is exactly why pinning it to your daily code check works so well.
